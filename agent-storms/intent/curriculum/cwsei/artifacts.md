Below is a **CWSEI-named artifact catalog**, with **(a) how to make it** (only where CWSEI sources in this project give explicit guidance) and **(b) a “not to do” list** when CWSEI explicitly provides one.  
For items that CWSEI only **lists in the Teaching Practices Inventory (TPI)** (i.e., it’s an inventory prompt, not a how-to), I’ll label them **“TPI item (no build guide in provided sources)”** and give the _most direct CWSEI-adjacent guidance available_ from the Course Transformation Guide / learning-science notes we have.

---

## Learning goals

### **course-level goals**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Write 5–10 big “At the end of this course, students will be able to…” statements with a concrete action + task. 
- Draft them from _what instructors already want students to do_: homework, exams, and downstream-course expectations. 
- Use verbs matched to the intended cognitive level (Bloom-aligned verb table). 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t let them stay “grandiose meta statements”; deconstruct into assessable performance. 
- Don’t design the goals based on wishful thinking; start by documenting what is currently assessed/taught, then refine. 

---

### **topic-level learning goals**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Create topic-level goals aligned to the course-level goals (“also known as learning outcomes/objectives”). 
- Build them “assessment-driven”: for each exam question, write “If a student gets this correct, they can…” 
- Then “lecture-driven”: walk topic-by-topic through what you cover and ask “afterwards the student can do what?” 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t over-index on only what’s on the final—acknowledge important abilities that aren’t yet tested, and treat that as a design gap to close. 

---

## Course information provided to students (TPI Section I)

### **List of topics to be covered**

**TPI item (no build guide in provided sources)**

- Derive from the _topic-level learning goals_ and make sequencing explicit (what connects to what) to reduce cognitive load. 

**Not to do (CWSEI-adjacent)**

- Don’t present topics as isolated “Chapter 4 in week 6” islands; avoid one-and-done coverage. 



---

### **List of topic-specific competencies (skills, expertise, …) students should achieve (what students should be able to do)**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- This is essentially your _topic-level learning goals_ in student-facing form: “students will be able to…” + action verb + task. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t wait until right before the midterm to reveal these—CWSEI examples show value in surfacing goals continuously (lecture-level goals). 



---

### **List of competencies that are not topic related (critical thinking, problem solving, …)**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Use “components of expert thinking” as the basis (identify relevant concepts, separate surface vs structure, plan solution, evaluate reasonableness, etc.). 
- Make practice+feedback explicit as the mechanism (multiple opportunities, scaffolding early, fade supports later). 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t rely on typical back-of-chapter problems alone; they remove many expert-thinking components (all info given, concept-chapter cues, etc.). 



---

### **Affective goals – changing students’ attitudes and beliefs (interest, motivation, relevance, beliefs about their competencies, how to master the material)**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- State affective aims explicitly (e.g., value, relevance, “how to learn more effectively”)—see PHYS 250 example. 
- Reinforce “effort + effective practice” culture (first-week framing). 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Avoid scare tactics (“many will fail”, “this is impossibly hard”); CWSEI flags this as demotivating.

## Pre-class work

### **Preclass-Reading Assignments**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Use reading to front-load definitions/vocabulary so class time can be higher-level. 
- Implement the “softer JITT” model: weekly reading + quiz before class; instructor adapts to difficulties “just in time.” 
- Key components CWSEI names: (1) specific reading, (2) quiz questions explicitly refer to the text, (3) don’t start class by repeating the reading. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t assign long unfocused readings; CWSEI calls that unrealistic for first exposure. 

---

### **pre-reading assignments**

Same implementation as **Preclass-Reading Assignments** (CWSEI uses both labels in the guide). 

---

### **quiz** / **pre-reading quiz questions**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Make it for marks (small % is enough; CWSEI reports completion rates with ~2–5% weighting). 
- Write questions that force opening the exact figure/section you care about (figure numbers, equations). 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t ask vague questions that can be answered without reading; tie prompts explicitly to the assigned pages/figures.

## In-class activities & engagement

### **clicker question**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Make clickers a regular, serious part of the course. 
- Use challenging conceptual questions with multiple plausible answers to trigger discussion. 
- Use peer instruction: answer individually → discuss → answer again → instructor follow-up that explains wrong answers too. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t use clickers as an “attendance/testing device”; CWSEI warns this yields little benefit and resentment. 
- Don’t limit yourself to only one-right-answer items; use “best answer / most efficient” style when appropriate. 
- Don’t turn policing (e.g., “multiple clickers”) into a class-wide focus; handle individually. 

---

### **Personal Response System (PRS)** / **Number of PRS questions posed followed by student-student discussion** / **Number of times PRS used as quiz device (counts for marks and no student discussion)**

**TPI item (build guidance comes from clicker guidance above)**

- If your goal is learning: prioritize student-student discussion + follow-up feedback. 
- If used as quiz device: keep it limited and don’t crowd out discussion-based use. (CWSEI emphasizes discussion for learning gains.) 



---

### **Student group work**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Pick the _level_ (brief in-class discussions vs informal study groups vs formal tutorials vs team projects) and design to match goals. 
- Requirements for success: small groups (2–4), explicit expectations/reward/accountability, and avoid groups composed solely of low motivation/low ability students. 
- For longer activities: assign roles (leader, recorder, reporter), and circulate/listen during work. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t assume group work is automatically beneficial; CWSEI explicitly notes potential downsides and the need to design for success. 
- Don’t choose products that force solo work (long writing, routine procedure application) for in-class group time; CWSEI recommends these as homework instead. 

---

### **In-class activities**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**
Use CWSEI’s design questions (from the “In-class activities” guidance):

- Ensure it’s challenging-but-doable in groups and connected to motivation/real world/career relevance. 
- Define a product students generate (decision + justification, prediction, ranking, judgment, novel representation). 
- Logistics: group size guidance (2–3 in fixed seats; “four-in-a-row doesn’t work”), assign roles for longer activities, make expectations explicit, circulate and listen. 

**Not to do (CWSEI – “What Not To Do”)**

- Don’t use active learning without explaining why you teach this way. 
- Don’t immediately tell the answer; let discussion happen first. 
- Don’t leave activities unresolved; provide a clear expert follow-up. 
- Don’t forget accountability (turn something in / clicker checkpoints / group reporting). 
- Don’t run activities that aren’t clearly targeting specific learning goals. 

---

### **Reflective activity at end of class, e.g. “one minute paper” or similar**

**TPI item (no dedicated build guide in provided sources)**

- Treat as retrieval + feedback: ask students to surface confusion (“what was hardest / muddiest point”) so instructor can adjust. This aligns with CWSEI’s “probe understanding and adjust.”

## Assignments + assessments

### **Problem sets/homework assigned and contributed to course grade at intervals of 2 weeks or less**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Design homework for: (1) challenging practice of expert thinking, (2) motivation, (3) feedback that guides improvement. 
- Build “attend-to-feedback” mechanisms (error explanations, reflection problems, and explicit alignment with exams). 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t rely on typical back-of-chapter problems that remove key expert-thinking components (all info given, no concept selection, etc.). 

---

### **homework question**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Write prompts that force students to reveal reasoning (concept choice, assumptions, plan, checks), not just a number. 
- Reduce grading load with structured components (computer-gradable concept selection, selective grading, or peer grading). 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t make the deliverable “a single number/fact” as the main output; CWSEI argues this hides thinking and prevents useful feedback. 

---

### **Midterm Exam(s)** / **Final Exam** / **exam question**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Align every exam item to learning goals and ensure students can see the connection (prevents “busy work” complaints). 
- Use “suites of questions” so the same learning goal is assessed across homework/clicker/exam with varied surface features. 
- Make exams cumulative to support retention. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t depend solely on exams for the course mark; CWSEI argues this leads to more naïve study and higher failure rates than courses with significant marked assignments. 

---

### **In-class quizzes** / **Online quizzes**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Use as frequent, smaller tasks that enable timely feedback (CWSEI calls feedback frequency/type “single most important element”). 
- Use them as part of preclass reading + just-in-time adjustments. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t give feedback too late to matter; CWSEI emphasizes feedback must be timely and actionable. 

---

### **Paper or project (an assignment taking longer than two weeks…)**

**TPI item (no dedicated build guide in provided sources)**

- Use “practice aimed at specific goals + rubrics” (CWSEI recommends explicit goals and rubrics to define criteria). 
- Provide examples of strong vs weak performance (CWSEI explicitly recommends positive and negative examples). 



---

### **Explicit group assignments**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Ensure assignments _require judgement/decisions_ to encourage real teamwork, and provide team roles + explicit teamwork expectations if grading collective work. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t grade collective work without teaching “why and how to work in teams effectively” and having a process for intra-group conflict. 

---

### **2-Stage exams**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Use the two-stage pattern: individual attempt → group attempt → instructor listens for widespread difficulties and addresses them. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t frame the first-stage as punitive; CWSEI recommends positioning it as “carefully designed practice” (especially for review/background). 

---

### **Peer assessment** / **Self assessment or reflection assignments**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Require students to act on feedback (error analysis / reflection prompt).
- Provide explicit guidelines to make student-to-student feedback valuable. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t assume students will naturally use feedback; add mechanisms that require attention and action. 

---

### **Students see assignment answer key and/or grading rubric** / **Students see midterm exam(s) answer key(s)**

**TPI item (build guidance: “Practice & Feedback” + homework doc)**

- Give criteria/rubrics so the task and marking standards are explicit.
- Provide solutions + require reflection/error explanation to force use of feedback. 



---

### **Assignments with feedback before grading or with opportunity to redo work to improve grade**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Build a redo path via structured reflection: allow points back for explaining incorrect thinking; include “reflection problems” in each homework set. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t provide feedback without a structure that makes students attend to it. 

---

## Course alignment / question-bank artifacts

### **Promoting course alignment: Developing a systematic approach to question development**

**How to make it (CWSEI — explicit steps)**

1. Choose a learning goal. 
2. Decide settings (lecture/homework/exam/lab/etc.). 
3. Write an initial application/prediction question. 
4. Identify variables you can change across questions. 
5. Create at least one exam + one clicker + one homework question aligned to the same goal. 

**Not to do (CWSEI)**

- Don’t let assessments feel discontinuous (“busy work”); alignment to well-defined learning goals is the fix. 

---

### **suites of questions**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Implement exactly as above (the guide explicitly defines “suites” as multi-setting variants for one learning goal). 

---

### **bank of questions**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Build the bank by repeatedly running the “suites” process, storing variants by learning goal + setting.

## Supporting materials (TPI Section II)

These are **TPI items**; CWSEI doesn’t give “how to create each material type” in a single recipe in the files we have, but you _can_ anchor them to CWSEI’s explicit learning-science constraints:

### **Solutions to homework assignments**

- Provide solutions and add “attend-to-feedback” mechanisms (error explanation, reflection prompts). 

### **Worked examples (text, pencast, or other format)**

- Use worked examples early to reduce cognitive load in initial problem solving. 

### **Practice or previous year’s exams**

- Make practice cumulative and spaced; encourage repeated retrieval/application. 

### **Animations, video clips, or simulations related to course material**

- Use them to reduce load / illustrate spatial relationships, but avoid “seductive details” that distract from learning. 

### **Lecture notes or course Powerpoint presentations (partial/skeletal or complete)**

- Explicitly show how topics are linked and the organization of the presentation (helps “chunking”). 

### **Student wikis or discussion boards…** (either item)

- If used for learning, add explicit guidelines and accountability; student-to-student feedback needs structure. 

(Each item above is listed in the TPI.)

## Measurement / improvement (TPI Section + CWSEI RFP methodology)

### **CWSEI Teaching Practices Inventory**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Fill it out as the course-practice record (it’s designed to take ~10 minutes). 

**Not to do**

- Don’t treat it as a “score”; CWSEI frames it as an inventory of practices that may or may not apply to every course. 

---

### **Assessment given at beginning of course to assess background knowledge**

**How to make it (CWSEI)**

- Use a low-stakes quiz + group stage (two-stage review) to reveal misconceptions and background variation; listen to group discussions to detect widespread difficulties. 
- CWSEI also recommends early assessment to set appropriate challenge levels and adjust criteria as students develop proficiency. 

---

### **Use of instructor-independent pre-post test (e.g. concept inventory) to measure learning**

**How to make it (CWSEI – explicit methodology)**

- Follow the multi-step development method CWSEI describes for machine-gradable instruments: faculty consensus → characterize student thinking (work, observations, interviews) → open-ended items → convert to MC → expert review → student interview validation → large-scale administration + stats → revise over time. 

---

### **Use of a consistent measure of learning that is repeated in multiple offerings…** / **Use of pre-post survey of student interest…** / **Opportunities for students’ self-evaluation of learning** / **New teaching methods… tried along with measurements…**

**TPI items (no detailed build recipe in provided sources)**

- The most direct CWSEI instruction we have is: _if you try methods/materials, measure impact_, and design assessments/feedback so they actually support learning (timely, specific, requires acting on feedback). 
- For self-evaluation specifically: require reflection on feedback and incorporation into later work.
